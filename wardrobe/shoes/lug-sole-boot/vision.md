# The Lug-Sole Combat Boot — Design Vision

*The trend-driven utility boot — Experimental tier, a single considered bet on an unvalidated silhouette. See [`../README.md`](../README.md) for its place in the full shoe catalogue and [`../../wardrobe-manifesto.md`](../../wardrobe-manifesto.md) for the governing design doctrine.*

---

## 1. Name & Philosophy

**The Lug-Sole Combat Boot.**

This is the direct inverse case of the Rain/Snow Boot, and worth naming that contrast explicitly rather than leaving it implicit: the Rain/Snow Boot is genuinely functional first, with zero aesthetic signaling — this piece is aesthetic/trend-driven first, borrowing the *visual language* of a functional utility boot (chunky lug sole, hardware-adjacent lacing) without needing the Rain/Snow Boot's actual seam-sealed waterproof certification. That's a real question worth resolving honestly rather than glossing over: does borrowing function's *look* without its full technical substance violate the manifesto's "structure earns its place, not decoration pretending to be mechanism" rule (see [`../../../objects/pen/vision.md`](../../../objects/pen/vision.md) Section 0 for where that exact phrase comes from)? The answer this piece commits to is no, on one condition — the lug sole has to deliver genuinely better traction and durability than a standard fashion-boot sole, even without full technical waterproofing. As long as the chunky tread is doing real grip-and-wear work, not just visual bulk, the "borrowed aesthetic" reads as an honest utility upgrade a wearer actually benefits from, not decoration pretending to be mechanism. This is exactly the kind of untested bet the Experimental tier exists for — build one, in the safest colorway, and find out whether it earns a permanent place or gets retired per the priority system's own trial-period rule.

## 2. Exact Specifications

| Spec | Target | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Heel height | 0.5–1" (13–25mm), near-flat to low block | A deliberate override of the "moderate heel worn often" rule, for aesthetic/genre reasons this time rather than the Rain/Snow Boot's safety reasons — a combat boot silhouette reads correctly at this height, not at Anchor Heel height |
| Outsole | Rubber lug, aggressive tread depth | Must deliver genuinely better grip and wear-durability than a standard fashion-boot sole — see Section 1's resolved tension |
| Silhouette | Chunky, ankle-to-mid-calf shaft height | Distinct from the Hug Boot and Rain/Snow Boot's Chelsea silhouettes — this is the catalogue's one combat/utility-inspired boot shape |
| Closure | Lace-up (6–8 eyelets) plus a functional inner side zip | See Section 4 — laces carry the genre's visual identity, the zip is the practical daily on/off answer |

## 3. Material Palette — One Option for the First Bet

Per the Experimental tier's own rule (build exactly one, in the safest colorway, before deciding whether to expand), this piece launches with a single material rather than the ranked multi-option palette used on validated-tier shoes:

- **Waxed or oiled leather** — a heavier, more utility-appropriate finish than the aniline nappa used elsewhere in the catalogue, but still a genuine patina-friendly finish, not a corrected-grain, static-forever leather. Wax/oil finishes darken and develop character at crease points with wear, satisfying the same "ages, doesn't stay static" rule the rest of the system holds to, just through a heavier-duty finish appropriate to this silhouette's utility register.

## 4. Construction Details

- **Lace-up plus functional zip:** the same combination logic as the Hug Boot's elastic-gusset-plus-zip (see [`../hug-boot/vision.md`](../hug-boot/vision.md) Section 4), adapted to a lace-up closure — laces are this genre's expected visual identity and shouldn't be removed to "simplify" the silhouette, but a functional inner zip is the honest daily-wear answer for a boot that needs to go on and off quickly, the same reasoning the Hug Boot already established.
- **Lug outsole:** genuinely deep tread, sourced as a real technical/utility outsole rather than a cosmetic lug pattern molded onto a standard fashion sole — this is the load-bearing decision behind the entire Section 1 argument, and it needs to be true in the actual manufactured product, not just implied by the silhouette.
- **Footbed:** cushioned, memory foam over EVA, same standard as the rest of the catalogue — an Experimental bet doesn't get a pass on the comfort rule any more than a going-out statement piece does.
- **Shaft:** ankle-to-mid-calf height, structured enough to hold its shape without a shearling or heavy insulated lining — this isn't a cold-weather technical boot like the Rain/Snow Boot, so the shaft is built for silhouette and support, not warmth.

## 5. Starlink One Reference — Quiet, In the Lace Hardware

- **Chain-link lace aglets:** the metal tips at the end of each lace are cast as small chain-link-shaped pieces instead of standard cylindrical aglets — a detail that's felt during lacing (handled directly, every time the boot goes on) more than it's seen from a normal viewing distance, the same "functional hardware carrying the reference" logic as the Hug Boot's pull tab (see [`../hug-boot/vision.md`](../hug-boot/vision.md) Section 5), scaled to this boot's more utilitarian hardware language.
- **No other exterior reference** — consistent with this being an Experimental, trend-driven piece rather than a statement piece; the quiet aglet detail is enough to satisfy the catalogue's "every pair has at least one" rule without turning an unvalidated bet into a hardware-forward showcase.

## 6. Sizing & Last Notes

- **True to size**, with room confirmed for a slightly thicker sock given the boot's utility register (though without the Rain/Snow Boot's insulation layer specifically).
- **Shaft fit at the calf** matters here the way it does on the (currently unbuilt) knee-high boot concept — confirm the shaft doesn't gape or pinch across a real range of calf circumferences.
- **Last grading, not linear scaling:** same rule as the rest of the catalogue for US 5–7.
- **Trial period:** per the priority system's Experimental rule, revisit this piece after a defined trial period (a season, or ~10 wears) and either promote it toward a validated tier or retire it — a documented, planned decision point, not an open-ended experiment.
