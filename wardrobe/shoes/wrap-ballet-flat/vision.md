# The Wrap Ballet Flat — Design Vision

*The dance-inspired ribbon-wrap flat — Medium tier, the most literal balletic reference in the catalogue. See [`../README.md`](../README.md) for its place in the full shoe catalogue and [`../../wardrobe-manifesto.md`](../../wardrobe-manifesto.md) for the governing design doctrine.*

---

## 1. Name & Philosophy

**The Wrap Ballet Flat.**

Two honest questions have to be answered before this piece earns a place in the catalogue rather than reading as a duplicate: the Perfect Flat already covers "everyday flat," and the Barely There Sandal and Espadrille Wedge already cover "ribbon-tie closure" — so what does this piece do that those don't? The answer is that it inverts the Perfect Flat's whole argument rather than repeating it. The Perfect Flat's identity is engineered-but-hidden — a concealed wedge, a raised loafer vamp, structure the wearer never sees (see [`../perfect-flat/vision.md`](../perfect-flat/vision.md) Section 1). This piece is the opposite bet: genuinely, visibly minimal, with almost no hidden engineering at all — the design signal here is "look how simple this is," not "look how much invisible structure makes this comfortable." And where the sandals' and wedge's ribbon ties are a summer/resort register, this piece's ribbon wraps up the ankle the way an actual ballet slipper's ribbons do — the most direct dance-costume reference anywhere in the system, distinct from that warm-weather ribbon language even though the underlying strap construction is shared. It sits at Medium rather than High for an honest reason too: a long ribbon wrap takes real time to tie correctly every single wear, which is a genuine cost against daily grab-and-go wearability — this is a romantic detail piece that reinforces the wardrobe's LoveShackFancy identity, not a workhorse competing with the Perfect Flat for everyday rotation.

## 2. Exact Specifications

| Spec | Target | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Heel height | None — a true flat, no internal wedge | Deliberately not the Perfect Flat's hidden 0.5" wedge (see [`../perfect-flat/vision.md`](../perfect-flat/vision.md) Section 4) — this piece's whole identity depends on being genuinely minimal, not engineered-but-disguised |
| Toe shape | Rounded, soft, closer to an actual ballet slipper toe than the Perfect Flat's more structured almond | Softest toe profile in the catalogue |
| Vamp height | Low — a true ballet-flat cut, not the Perfect Flat's raised loafer-hybrid vamp | The visible contrast point between the two flats in this catalogue |
| Ribbon length | Long enough to wrap the ankle once or twice and tie, wearer's choice | See Section 6 — adjustable wrap count is genre-accurate to real ribbon-tie ballet flats, not a design shortcut |
| Sole | Thin, flexible | Closer to an actual dance-shoe sole than any other flat in this catalogue |

## 3. Material Palette — Two Options

### 1st — Aniline Nappa Calfskin (flagship)
- **Finish:** vegetable-tanned or veg-chrome combination, full aniline dye, no corrected grain — the same flagship rule as the rest of the catalogue, extended here to the softest, most slipper-like leather weight in the system.

### 2nd — Cotton Twill or Canvas
- **Construction:** soft, lightweight cotton twill or canvas — a genuine dance-class-inspired option, breathable and casual.
- **Why it's ranked second, not lesser:** the more literal "ballet slipper" material read of the two, offered for wearers who want the silhouette at its most minimal and unstructured.

## 4. Construction Details

- **Construction method: slip-lasted or cement construction**, the same lightweight method the Perfect Flat uses (see [`../perfect-flat/vision.md`](../perfect-flat/vision.md) Section 4) rather than anything closer to Blake or Goodyear — this piece needs the thinnest, most flexible sole in the catalogue, genuinely closer to a dance-shoe build than a street-shoe one.
- **No hidden wedge, no raised vamp:** the deliberate absence of both Perfect Flat signatures — see Section 1. What structure exists is limited to what a real ballet flat needs to hold its shape and support the foot, nothing added for silhouette engineering.
- **Ribbon wrap attachment:** ribbon ties are anchored through a small series of loops or flat D-rings set into the topline of the shoe at the heel and both sides of the ankle opening — not sewn directly and permanently into one fixed wrap pattern, so the wearer can genuinely choose a single wrap (faster, more casual) or a double wrap (more dramatic, more literal ballet-slipper reference) each time she puts them on.
- **Ribbon construction:** the same hybrid leather/fabric-core ribbon construction already validated for the Barely There Sandal and Espadrille Wedge (see [`../barely-there-sandal/vision.md`](../barely-there-sandal/vision.md) Section 4) — a soft grosgrain ribbon face bonded to a structural core, necessary here more than almost anywhere else in the catalogue given how much daily tying, untying, and retying this specific ribbon sees compared to a single ankle-wrap sandal strap.
- **Footbed:** a thin memory foam layer over a soft base — genuinely minimal, but never zero cushioning; comfort stays non-negotiable even on the catalogue's most stripped-down silhouette.

## 5. Starlink One Reference — The Ribbon Tie Ends

Reusing the exact precedent the Espadrille Wedge already established, rather than inventing a new placement for a shoe that's already carrying this system's most literal ribbon detail:

- **Ribbon tie ends:** a fine Starlink Emerald (`#0F5C3D`, see [`../../color-system.md`](../../color-system.md)) thread woven into the very ends of each ribbon — visible only when the ribbon is untied or being tied, the same "discovered in a private moment" logic as the Espadrille Wedge (see [`../espadrille-wedge/vision.md`](../espadrille-wedge/vision.md) Section 5), which is itself this piece's closest sibling in construction language even though the two read differently at the ankle.
- **No hardware, no color accent on the shoe body itself** — the ribbon is already this piece's entire visual argument; adding a second detail elsewhere would compete with it rather than support it.

## 6. Wrap Configuration — Wearer's Choice, Not a Fixed Pattern

- **Single wrap:** the ribbon crosses the ankle once and ties at the side or back — faster to put on, more casual, closer to daily wearability.
- **Double wrap:** the ribbon crosses the ankle twice before tying — the more dramatic, more literal ballet-slipper read, better suited to occasions where the extra few minutes of tying is worth it.
- This isn't two separate builds the way the Barely There Sandal's Everyday Strap and Dressy Slide are — it's one ribbon, long enough to do either, with the choice left to the wearer each time. The tradeoff named in Section 1 (daily-wearability cost of a wrap closure) is real regardless of which configuration is chosen; this just lets the wearer decide how much of that cost to take on a given day.

## 7. Sizing & Last Notes

- **True to size.**
- **Ribbon length must accommodate a genuine range of ankle circumferences at both wrap counts**, the same fit-safety consideration the Espadrille Wedge names for its own ankle-tie (see [`../espadrille-wedge/vision.md`](../espadrille-wedge/vision.md) Section 7) — confirm excess length at a single wrap doesn't become a trip hazard, and confirm a double wrap doesn't run short on a larger ankle.
- **Last grading, not linear scaling:** same rule as the rest of the catalogue for US 5–7.
- **The softest, least structured last in the catalogue:** confirm the last-maker understands this shoe is meant to feel closer to a dance slipper than a street shoe — a last graded with the same structural assumptions as the Perfect Flat would undercut the whole point of this piece.
