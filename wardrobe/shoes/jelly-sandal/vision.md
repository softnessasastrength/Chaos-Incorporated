# The Jelly Sandal — Design Vision

*The poolside/festival jelly sandal — High tier, and the one shoe in the catalogue built on a confirmed personal favorite rather than a named gap or an untested bet. See [`../README.md`](../README.md) for its place in the full shoe catalogue and [`../../wardrobe-manifesto.md`](../../wardrobe-manifesto.md) for the governing design doctrine.*

---

## 1. Name & Philosophy

**The Jelly Sandal.**

This piece sits at High rather than Experimental for an unusual reason in this catalogue: every other High-tier item earns its place through a named functional gap (the Espadrille Wedge's warm-weather elevation, the Horsebit Loafer's structured-loafer gap), but this one earns it through a confirmed, already-loved category — she already reaches for jelly sandals, which is a stronger signal than any Experimental bet gets to start with, even though the material itself is genuinely new territory for this system. That's worth being honest about rather than smoothing over: this is the first shoe in the entire catalogue built from injection-molded PVC/TPU rather than leather, suede, canvas, or jute, and it's the first piece anywhere in this system that breaks — not bends — the "materials chosen to patina, not stay static" rule every other item follows. PVC doesn't age into character the way aniline leather or a jute wedge does; it just yellows and eventually degrades under UV. Rather than quietly ignoring that, this piece resolves the tension the same way the Rain/Snow Boot resolved its own aniline-leather exception: by naming it directly and finding the one thing this material can do that leather can't. That thing is color *as* the material itself — a translucent Starlink Emerald jelly doesn't put the ring's color on the shoe, it makes the shoe the ring's color, all the way through. That's the most literal single-object echo of Starlink One anywhere in this catalogue, and it's only possible in a material this system has never used before.

## 3. Material Palette — Compound, Not Leather

Unlike every other item in this catalogue, there's no ranked leather/suede/canvas palette here — the entire material question is which PVC/TPU compound and which color:

- **Compound: flexible, soft-touch PVC or TPU** — deliberately not the rigid, glass-hard PVC classic vintage jelly sandals are known for. This is the direct answer to the "Warm Hug demands comfort, jellies are famously uncomfortable" tension: a softer, more flexible compound at a lower Shore A hardness than a traditional jelly, so the straps flex with the foot instead of digging in.
- **Colorway 1 — Starlink Emerald (translucent), flagship:** the material itself tinted to the ring's own `#0F5C3D` (see [`../../color-system.md`](../../color-system.md)), fully translucent so the color reads through the whole shoe, not as a surface coating. This is the piece's reason for existing as a Starlink One reference — see Section 5.
- **Colorway 2 — Cloud Ivory (frosted/opaque):** a quieter, milky-frosted finish for wearers who want the silhouette without the saturated color statement.
- **Colorway 3 — Crystal Clear (fully transparent, uncolored):** playful and genre-classic — lets a pedicure or a fine ankle chain show through, the one colorway with no color story of its own.

## 4. Construction Details

- **Molded construction, not stitched:** this is a genuinely different manufacturing category from every other shoe in this catalogue — injection-molded PVC/TPU rather than cut-and-sewn leather or textile. There's no upper-to-sole attachment step the way a leather shoe has; strap, footbed, and sole can be a single continuous molded piece, or built via a multi-shot mold — see `manufacturing-brief.md` Section 3 for the sourcing implications, which are closer in kind to the Yoga/Recovery Shoe's knit-upper sourcing question or the Rain/Snow Boot's technical-line question than to anything else in the catalogue.
- **Comfort layer — the direct answer to "jellies are uncomfortable":** a co-molded softer-compound zone at the footbed (a lower-Shore-A insert integrated into the mold, not a separately bonded foam layer, since foam doesn't bond reliably to flexible PVC/TPU the way it bonds to leather or EVA) — this is what lets this piece meet the catalogue's comfort-non-negotiable standard despite the material genre's reputation.
- **Edge finishing:** all strap and footbed edges rounded and deflashed at the mold-tooling stage — since there's no fabric or leather edge to finish afterward the way every other shoe in this catalogue allows, edge comfort has to be solved entirely in the mold design itself, not corrected in a later finishing step.
- **Hardware, if a buckle closure is used:** small silver-tone metal buckle, per the system-wide hardware rule — everything else on this shoe is plastic, so any metal touchpoint stays consistent with the rest of the system rather than introducing a separate finish language.

## 5. Starlink One Reference — The Material Itself

**The most literal single-object echo of the ring in the entire catalogue**, and a genuinely different kind of reference than anywhere else in this system:

- Every other piece puts the ring's color *on* something — a thread, a satin dye, an enamel inset, a lace aglet. This piece's Starlink Emerald colorway makes the color the shoe's actual substance, translucent all the way through, the way the ring's own emerald is a solid stone you see all the way into rather than a surface tint.
- **This still follows existing precedent, not a new rule:** the Cloud Walker and Anchor Heel both already offer a Starlink Emerald colorway alongside quieter neutral options at non-statement tiers (see [`../cloud-walker/vision.md`](../cloud-walker/vision.md) Section 5 and [`../anchor-heel/vision.md`](../anchor-heel/vision.md) Section 6) — a colorway option has never been treated as an overt-hardware violation of the "quiet on everyday/High-tier pieces" rule in this system, only literal hardware shape (chain-link forms) has been reserved for statement tiers or genre-expected contexts like the Horsebit Loafer. This piece's emerald colorway sits comfortably inside that existing precedent, just taken to its most literal conclusion a translucent material happens to make possible.
- **Cloud Ivory and Crystal Clear carry no reference** — consistent with every other item's "quiet colorways stay quiet" pattern.

## 6. Sizing & Last Notes

- **PVC/TPU doesn't break in the way leather does** — there's no "softens after a few wears" story here; the strap has to fit correctly and comfortably in the first wear, the same standard the Statement Mule and Kitten Heel Mary Jane hold their structural straps to, but for a different reason (no break-in period exists for this material at all, not because the strap is load-bearing).
- **Flexible compound gives some fit forgiveness across widths** that a rigid material wouldn't, but this shouldn't be assumed without a physical sample — confirm actual give against a real foot-width range before finalizing a single-width-only production plan.
- **Last/mold grading, not linear scaling:** the same US 5–7 independent-grading rule applies here even though "last" means a shoe mold rather than a traditional shoe last — the underlying reason (small feet aren't just scaled-down big feet) doesn't change with the manufacturing method.
