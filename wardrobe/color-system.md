# Chaos Incorporated Color System

*Signature palette for Renn's wardrobe. Every item's colorway options should be pulled from this list unless there's a documented, specific reason to break it (see "Breaking the Palette" below).*

---

## Why a Closed Palette

A wardrobe with an unlimited color range doesn't mix — every outfit becomes a one-off negotiation. A closed, deliberate palette means any top can pair with any bottom and it works by default. This is the same logic as the manifesto's structure principle applied to color: **fewer, better-chosen colors create more usable outfits than a wide, undisciplined range.**

Two things anchor every choice below:
- **Skin undertone: cool.** Colors are chosen to sit correctly against cool undertones — no orange-leaning warm neutrals, no yellow-gold metallics. Where a warm color shows up (see Sage, Blush Mauve) it's a cooled-down, muted version of that color, not a true warm.
- **Starlink One is the fixed reference point.** Sterling silver is the only metal in this system — all hardware, zippers, buttons, buckles, and jewelry default to silver/gunmetal/pewter finishes, never gold or rose gold. Emerald green is the anchor color the rest of the palette is built around.

That's 9 colors — inside the 6–10 target, with room to drop one (most likely Jet Black, the least-used) if the palette ever needs tightening further.

## How the Colors Split by Function

- **Anchor (1):** Starlink Emerald — the ring color, used sparingly and deliberately so it stays special.
- **Base neutrals (2):** Cloud Ivory, Sterling Grey — do the most work, appear in the highest volume of pieces (basics, leggings, denim washes read as neutral).
- **Signature romantic colors (3):** Blush Mauve, Periwinkle, Lavender Mist — this is where the LoveShackFancy x Target whimsy lives. Florals, prints, and trim details draw from this trio.
- **Secondary green (1):** Sage — bridges the neutrals and the anchor; keeps green in rotation without repeating the exact emerald everywhere.
- **Grounding neutrals (2):** Midnight Navy, Jet Black — structure pieces, denim, outerwear, anything that needs to recede and let a signature color or the ring lead.

## Pairing Logic With Starlink One

The ring is small, saturated, and metal-forward — it needs to read as a highlight, not get lost or fight for attention.

- **Best pairings:** Cloud Ivory, Sterling Grey, Blush Mauve, and Lavender Mist as the outfit base — these are quiet enough that the emerald and the silver chain-link detail stay the visual focal point on the hand.
- **Confident pairings:** Sage or Starlink Emerald itself worn elsewhere in the look (bag, shoe, hair ribbon) creates tonal echo rather than competition — same color family, different saturation.
- **Use with intention, not as a base:** Periwinkle and Midnight Navy are saturated/dark enough to pull focus if worn as a large block near the hands (e.g., a sleeve). Fine as a base layer elsewhere in the outfit; keep the hand/wrist area (cuffs, sleeve trim) in a quieter color when the ring is meant to be the detail people notice.
- **Never:** gold, rose gold, or warm brass hardware anywhere in the system — it will visibly clash with both the cool undertone and the sterling silver of the ring.

## Translucent Color (Soft Light)

Sheer and translucent garments use these same nine colors as **light filters**, not a separate palette. Opacity zones, one-loud-Emerald rules, and layering requirements live in [`translucent-system.md`](translucent-system.md). Soft Structure jelly footwear uses translucent palette colorways under the same closed-list discipline — see [`shoes/jelly/README.md`](shoes/jelly/README.md).

## Breaking the Palette

The palette is closed but not a prison. A piece can use an off-palette color when:
1. It's a Phase 3 / Experimental statement piece explicitly testing a new color direction (document this in the item's Chaos Incorporated Notes), or
2. It's a print/pattern where the *ground* color is on-palette and secondary print colors range slightly wider (florals rarely stay in 9 colors and shouldn't be forced to).

Basics, denim, and Must-Have tier items should not break palette — that's what makes the "everything mixes" promise hold.
