# The Chaos Incorporated Wardrobe Manifesto

*Design doctrine for Renn's wardrobe system. Read this before adding, cutting, or approving anything.*

---

## I. The Thesis

Chaos Incorporated exists because "pick an aesthetic" is the wrong instruction. The right instruction is: **build a system where softness and structure hold each other up.**

Starlink One — the heart-emerald ring with the chain-link motif — is the whole philosophy in one object. A soft shape (the heart) is held in place by hard architecture (four engineered prongs, a chain-link lattice). The metal is left unprotected on purpose, so it tarnishes and develops character instead of staying showroom-perfect. Nothing about that ring is accidental, and nothing about this wardrobe should be either.

That's the model for every garment in this system:
- **Soft materials, deliberate structure.** Draping jersey and unstructured knits are welcome — but they pair with something engineered (a waistband that actually holds, a seam that's boned or reinforced, hardware that means something).
- **Comfort is not the enemy of the aesthetic. It is the aesthetic.** A piece that looks incredible and feels wrong gets cut, no exceptions.
- **Wear it in, don't varnish it.** Like the ring's intentional patina, denim washes, raw hems, and broken-in fabric hand are preferred over anything that reads as stiff or precious. Things are allowed to look lived-in.
- **Controlled chaos, not literal chaos.** Mixed textures, asymmetric details, unexpected hardware — yes. Garments that don't fit, fabrics that fight the body, silhouettes borrowed from someone else's proportions — no. The chaos is a design choice layered on top of engineering discipline, never a substitute for it.

## II. The Frame We're Designing For

Every fit decision in this system is engineered around a **64" (5'4") adult frame.** This is a proportion problem, not an afterthought, and it drives real technical decisions:

- **Rise matters more than it does on a taller frame.** A high or mid-rise sits at the natural waist and keeps the leg line long; low-rise on a shorter torso reads as cutting the body in half and is used sparingly and deliberately, not by default.
- **Inseam length is a first-class spec, not a rounding error.** Every bottoms item in this system documents inseam and notes whether it needs hemming/cropping — "one size, unhemmed, figure it out" is not acceptable at Chaos Incorporated.
- **Volume needs an exit strategy.** Wide-leg, baggy, and balloon silhouettes are fully welcome, but each one must specify how it avoids swallowing a shorter frame (cropped length, cinched waist, tucked-in proportion, heel height assumption).
- **Fabric weight is chosen for drape at this scale.** Heavy, stiff fabric behaves differently at 5'4" than at 5'9" — it moves less, structures the fold-line closer to the body, and can add visual bulk faster. Every fabric recommendation accounts for this.
- **Comfort is non-negotiable and specific, not vibes-based.** "Comfortable" isn't a checkbox — every item documents stretch %, fabric hand-feel, and where on the body it needs to move freely (waistband, crotch curve, knee, hem).

## III. Design Principles (the actual rules)

1. **Structure earns its place.** Boning, hardware, reinforced seams, and engineered waistbands are used because they do something (support, shape, security) — never purely decorative stiffness.
2. **Every soft piece needs one hard detail, and vice versa.** A flowy top wants a structured waistband or a hardware closure. A structured jacket wants a soft under-layer. Nothing is 100% soft or 100% hard.
3. **Patina over polish.** Default to washes, finishes, and materials that improve with age and wear. Avoid anything that only looks good in its first week.
4. **Fit before print.** Silhouette, rise, and proportion decisions come before pattern, wash, or color decisions. A great print on the wrong cut is still the wrong cut.
5. **Document the skin-feel, not just the spec sheet.** Fabric composition percentages are necessary but not sufficient — every item description should say what it actually feels like against her skin (soft-brushed, cool, structured-but-forgiving, etc.), because that's the difference between a piece she wears once and one she reaches for constantly.
6. **Pairing is part of the design, not an afterthought.** Nothing is designed in isolation — every item names what it's meant to be worn with, including how it sits alongside signature pieces like Starlink One.
7. **Priority tiers are honest, not aspirational.** Must-Have means she will wear this weekly and the wardrobe is incomplete without it. Experimental means it's a bet, not a promise — see Section IV.

## IV. Priority System

See [`priority-system.md`](priority-system.md) for the full rubric. Short version:

| Tier | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Must-Have** | Foundational. Worn in heavy weekly rotation. The wardrobe is incomplete without it. |
| **High** | Strong, validated addition. Fills a real gap or elevates an existing category. |
| **Medium** | Good idea, not urgent. Build once the Must-Have/High layer is solid. |
| **Experimental** | A bet on a new silhouette, fabric, or aesthetic direction. Small quantity, watch how it performs before scaling. |

## V. What Every Item Must Document

See [`item-template.md`](item-template.md). At minimum, every entry answers:
- Why it works for a 64" frame specifically (not generic sizing advice)
- Technical measurements (rise, inseam, stretch %, fabric weight)
- Fabric composition and skin-feel
- Colorways
- Sourcing (real product examples where possible)
- Styling/pairing logic
- Chaos Incorporated-specific modifications or aesthetic notes

## VI. Scope Note

This manifesto governs outerwear, tops, bottoms, shoes, accessories, and full-looks. It does not govern intimate/sleepwear categories, which are handled separately and conservatively — see the note in the repo root README if that section is ever built out.
